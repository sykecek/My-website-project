QUASARS

A quasar is a type of active galactic nucleus (AGN). When gas falls toward a supermassive black hole, it forms a hot accretion disk. Friction and magnetic processes heat the disk so intensely that it can outshine the entire host galaxy. That extreme luminosity is what makes quasars visible across huge cosmic distances.

Quasars were more common in the early universe, when galaxies had more gas available and black holes were growing quickly. Studying quasars therefore helps astronomers learn about the formation of galaxies and black holes in the young universe.

Connections to galaxy evolution

  • Black hole growth: quasars mark phases of rapid accretion.
  • Feedback: winds and jets can heat or expel gas, affecting star formation.
  • Mergers: collisions can funnel gas to the center and trigger AGN activity.
  • Cosmic history: quasars trace how structure formed over time.

Even though a quasar is “a galaxy with an extremely active center,” the quasar light can dominate what we see, making the host galaxy difficult to observe directly.

How quasars change over time

Quasar activity is usually a phase in a galaxy’s life. When enough gas reaches the central black hole, the nucleus can shine intensely for a time, but feedback from radiation, winds, and jets can heat or expel gas and eventually reduce the accretion rate.
