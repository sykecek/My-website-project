DWARF GALAXIES

Dwarf galaxies can contain from millions up to a few billion stars, but many are dominated by dark matter. Because they have weaker gravity, they are sensitive to feedback from supernovae and to environmental effects like tidal stripping and gas removal.

In the hierarchical model, large galaxies grow partly by absorbing smaller galaxies. This means dwarfs are not only common today — they also represent an important “building block” in the long-term assembly of galaxies.

Why dwarfs matter

  • Building blocks: many dwarfs are accreted by larger galaxies over time.
  • Dark matter labs: their dynamics help test how dark matter behaves on small scales.
  • Environmental effects: they can be stripped and quenched by nearby massive galaxies.
  • Early-universe clues: some dwarfs preserve very old stellar populations.

Some dwarf galaxies are satellites orbiting larger galaxies, while others are more isolated. Their diversity makes them crucial for understanding galaxy evolution across different environments.

How dwarfs change over time

Because dwarf galaxies have shallow gravitational wells, they are easily affected by supernova feedback and by their environment. Near larger galaxies, dwarfs can be stripped of gas and stars and gradually quenched, or they can be disrupted and accreted into the halos of bigger systems.
