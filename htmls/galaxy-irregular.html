IRREGULAR GALAXIES

“Irregular” does not mean “random” in a careless sense. It means the galaxy’s visible structure does not fit the classic spiral/elliptical categories. Some irregular galaxies are naturally small and clumpy. Others look irregular because they have been disturbed by gravitational encounters or are in the middle of merging.

Irregular galaxies are often gas-rich. That fuel can produce bright regions of star formation and many hot, short-lived stars, which makes some irregulars appear very blue.

Common causes of irregular structure

  • Tidal interactions: a close pass can pull gas and stars into tails and bridges.
  • Mergers: colliding galaxies can temporarily look chaotic while they settle.
  • Low mass: small galaxies can be easily stirred up by nearby neighbors.
  • Feedback: supernova-driven winds can reshape gas in a shallow gravitational potential.

Because they react strongly to environment, irregular galaxies are a useful “laboratory” for studying how gravity and star formation shape galaxies.

How irregulars change over time

Irregular galaxies can evolve quickly when they interact with neighbors. Close passes and mergers can reshape their gas and stars, triggering bursts of star formation and leaving behind tidal tails or disturbed, clumpy structure.
