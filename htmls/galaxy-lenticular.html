LENTICULAR GALAXIES

Lenticular galaxies typically show a bright central bulge surrounded by a disk, but they lack the prominent spiral arms seen in spiral galaxies. Many contain less cold gas than spirals, so their star formation is usually reduced.

One common idea is that lenticulars can form when a spiral galaxy loses its gas supply — for example, in a galaxy cluster where hot gas and interactions can strip away the fuel needed to form new stars. Over time, the disk remains, but the spiral pattern fades and the galaxy becomes smoother.

Formation pathways (examples)

  • Gas stripping: the environment removes gas, lowering star formation in the disk.
  • Minor mergers: repeated small mergers can thicken and heat the disk.
  • Internal evolution: bars and resonances can redistribute gas and stars.

Lenticular galaxies are especially common in dense environments, supporting the idea that surroundings can drive galaxy transformation.

How lenticulars change over time

Lenticular galaxies are often thought to be “faded spirals.” If a disk galaxy loses its gas supply (for example in clusters), star formation declines and the disk becomes smoother. Over time, minor mergers and internal processes can further thicken and heat the disk.
