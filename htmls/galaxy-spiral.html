SPIRAL GALAXIES

Spiral galaxies are disk-shaped galaxies with graceful spiral arms. They are rich in gas and dust, which makes them active sites of star formation.

A typical spiral galaxy has a flat, rotating disk, a central bulge, and a more extended halo. The spiral arms are not rigid “structures” made of the same stars forever. Instead, the arms are often explained as regions where gas and stars bunch up (like a traffic jam), compressing gas clouds and triggering new star formation as material orbits around the center.

Because the disk contains lots of cold gas, spirals often appear bluer than ellipticals: massive, short-lived blue stars light up the arms. Dust lanes can also be prominent, especially when we see a spiral galaxy edge-on.

Key features

  • Disk rotation: stars and gas orbit the center in an ordered way.
  • Spiral arms: enhanced star formation and bright young stellar populations.
  • Bulge: a dense central region, often with older stars and a supermassive black hole.
  • Halo: a spherical component with older stars, globular clusters, and dark matter.

Our Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy: it likely has a central bar-shaped structure that influences how gas flows inward and where new stars form.

How spirals change over time

Spiral galaxies can evolve through gas accretion, internal processes (like bars), and interactions with other galaxies. Close encounters can thicken the disk, create tidal tails, or even transform a spiral into a more spheroidal system after a major merger. Environment matters: spirals in dense clusters can lose gas more easily, reducing star formation.
