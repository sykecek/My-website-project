ELLIPTICAL GALAXIES

Elliptical galaxies range from small, faint ellipticals to the most massive galaxies known. Unlike spirals, their light distribution is typically smooth and their stellar motions are more random than ordered. Many ellipticals look “redder” because they are dominated by older, cooler stars.

In the hierarchical picture of galaxy formation, many ellipticals form (or are strongly reshaped) through mergers. Major mergers can disrupt disks, scramble stellar orbits, and create a more spheroidal remnant. The most massive ellipticals often live in galaxy groups and clusters, where interactions and mergers are common over cosmic time.

Typical properties

  • Smooth appearance: little visible structure like arms or dust lanes.
  • Older stars: less ongoing star formation in many systems.
  • Hot gas: massive ellipticals can hold very hot, X-ray-emitting gas.
  • Massive black holes: central supermassive black holes are common.

Even if an elliptical has little cold gas, it can still be active in other wavelengths because of hot gas halos and central black hole activity.

Why they are important

Elliptical galaxies help astronomers study how galaxies grow by merging and how star formation can be shut down. Their old stellar populations preserve clues about the early universe, and their environments (often dense clusters) show how surroundings influence galaxy evolution.
